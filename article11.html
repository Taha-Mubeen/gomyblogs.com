1. Siamese Fighting Fish (Betta Splendens):

The Siamese fighting fish (Betta Splendens), or Betta, is a freshwater fish native to Southeast Asia, specifically Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar, Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand, and Vietnam. Betta fish are among the world's most popular and beautiful pet fish. While the genus Betta contains 73 species, only Betta Splendens are eponymously known as "bettas," owing to their global popularity as a pet: they are among the most widely available aquarium fish in the world, owing to their varied and vibrant color. Betta fish are native to Asia, where they live in swamps, ponds, and slow-moving streams. Male bettas are the devoted fathers who use their mouths to create bubble nests for their young and fiercely defend them from prey. Betta fish, like us, are diurnal. That's why they are active during the day and sleep at night, necessitating the use of darkness to get a good night's rest. While some bettas are captured in the wild, the vast majority of those sold in the United States come from breeding farms in Thailand and other Southeast Asian countries, where they are commonly kept in small bottles. Fish are haphazardly dumped into baskets covered with nets and scooped up into small plastic cups for transport to the United States.

Betta fish can be frustrated and depressed. While most of them can be content in a tank on their own, their environment still requires a lot of enrichment, such as caves, plants, and space to explore. They are also stressed, especially when their fins are picked at by other incompatible fish in the same tank. Female bettas may be able to live in a peaceful group if they have enough space and hiding places. While males will fight other males and male gouramis (a similar-looking species), they may be able to live alone in a "community" aquarium with other docile fish species such as Cory catfish, and kuhli loaches, or guppies. They can coexist with nonfish species such as snails, ghost shrimp, and frogs. If aggression becomes a problem in any community tank, keep and maintain a separate, working quarantine aquarium where individuals can be placed to avoid conflict with other fish. Bettas are known to recognize humans, will follow your finger around the aquarium, and can even be trained to perform tricks! Betta fish have a life span of 3 to 5 years on average, but in rare cases, they can live up to 8 or 9 years in captivity. Bettas have a variety of tail shapes, the most common of which is the "veil tail." The "half-moon," "double tail," "short-finned fighting-style tail," and "crown tail" are other tail shapes.

2. ClownFish:

The clownfish, best known for its appearances in the films "Finding Nemo" and "Finding Dory," has become a popular aquarium pet. Nemo and his father, Marlin, are ocellaris clownfish, also known as clown anemonefish or false clownfish. Anemonefish get their name from the sea urchins that they live in. There are 28 anemonefish species, and they come in a variety of colors, including pink, red, yellow, black, brown, and multi-colored stripes. The fish's bright color, combined with those stunning, thick white bands, makes it one of the most easily recognized fish. The majority of false clownfish are orange in color with three white bands on the head and body. The white bands are black-outlined. According to the Animal Diversity Web, their bodies are slightly longer than 3 inches (88 millimeters) on average, but they can grow up to 4 inches (110 mm) (ADW). The tail is rounded, and the dorsal fin has 11 spines. Before taking up residence, clownfish perform an elaborate dance with an anemone, gently touching its tentacles with various parts of their bodies until they are adjusted to their host. A layer of mucus on the clownfish's skin protects it from the lethal sting of the fish-eating anemone. In exchange for protection from predators and food scraps, the clownfish chases away intruders and parasites by preening on its host.

They can change gender, but will only do so to become the dominant female in a group. The transformation is irreversible. Clownfish are found in the Pacific and Indian Oceans, where they live in the warm waters of sheltered reefs and shallow seas. To stay warm during the winter, they will migrate to deeper water. Clownfish are omnivorous creatures. They feed on dead anemone tentacles, anemone leftovers, plankton, mollusks, zooplankton, phytoplankton, small crustaceans, and various algae. Male clownfish are devoted. They will clean the nest, guard the eggs, and prepare the nest for the female. Clownfish communicate by popping and clicking their fins. Clownfish feed by swimming onto the reef, attracting larger fish, and luring them back to the anemone. The larger fish will be stung and eaten by the anemone, leaving the remains for the clownfish.
